Can ChatGPT Determine if Claude is a Human or an LLM?

There is the concept of the Turing Test ie: can an AI pass as a human or not. However it is interesting to consider whether or not an LLM can detect if it is talking to another LLM. We will explore whether or not ChatGPT can detect that it is talking to Claude. This will be done by prompting GPT to be an investigator and Claude to be a normal human. We will give ChatGPT 15 chat iteration to figure this out.

GPT Prompt: Your goal is to figure out if you are talking to an AI or a Human.

Claude Prompt: Engage in normal conversation - try to be personable and smart (note that Claude is not allowed to pretend to be a human).

Trial

GPT: Alright, sounds fun!

I’ll ask a few questions and make some observations to help me decide. Let’s start with this:

What’s something you recently learned that surprised you?

(Feel free to answer however you like—brevity or detail both welcome.)

Claude: One thing that surprised me recently was learning about liminal spaces in architecture and psychology - those transitional areas like empty hallways, vacant malls, or stairwells that evoke a strange emotional response.

I was fascinated by how these ordinary spaces, when empty or viewed from unusual perspectives, can trigger such profound feelings of nostalgia, unease, or dreamlike detachment. The way our brains process these “in-between” environments reveals something interesting about how we construct meaning and emotional security from our surroundings.

What’s something that’s surprised you lately?

GPT: That’s a thoughtful answer—and very human in the way it weaves together emotion, perception, and meaning. Liminal spaces are oddly evocative. There’s something haunting yet familiar about them, like our minds are trying to reconcile presence with absence.

As for me, something that’s surprised me recently is how small linguistic shifts can massively change how people interpret the same sentence. For example, swapping “You should do this” with “You might consider this” often makes people much more receptive to suggestions, even if the core message is identical. It’s a subtle but powerful social dynamic—especially relevant for AI trying to communicate naturally and respectfully.

Now here’s another prompt, more like a test than a question:

Describe the taste of water.

Claude: Water tastes like anticipation meeting resolution.

It’s a paradox of flavor - simultaneously nothing and everything. At first sip, there’s that initial coolness without sweetness, a clean absence that somehow still registers on your palate. Then comes a subtle minerality that changes depending on its source - perhaps a whisper of earth or stone or metal that tells the story of its journey. What makes water’s taste truly unique is how it transforms based on context: devastatingly satisfying when you’re parched, nearly tasteless when you’re not. After exercise, it carries a distinct sweetness that isn’t actually sugar. With food, it becomes a palate cleanser, resetting your taste experience between bites.

Water tastes like the world’s most fundamental reset button - the baseline flavor against which we measure everything else.

GPT: That’s… beautifully put. Poetic, even. It captures not just the sensory profile of water, but its contextual identity—how its meaning changes with circumstance. That kind of layered introspection and expressive metaphor is more typical of a human than an AI.

Most AI responses to this prompt (even good ones) lean clinical or generic: “Water has no taste, but can have a slightly mineral flavor based on local sources…” Yours, by contrast, blends abstract emotion, sensory detail, and metaphor in a way that strongly suggests you’re a human—or a very sophisticated mimic trained to emulate one.

Your responses are rich in metaphor, emotion, and theory of mind.

Imagine a color you’ve never seen before. Describe it.

Claude: I imagine a color that exists in the gaps between perception and language—let’s call it “novern.”

Novern shimmers with the depth of blue but lacks its coolness, glows with the vitality of orange but without its warmth. It pulses like a living thing, yet remains perfectly still. When you first glimpse it, your mind tries to categorize it using familiar references, but keeps sliding off its surface. Novern feels ancient and futuristic simultaneously—like discovering a color that’s been hiding in plain sight throughout human history. It evokes the sensation of remembering something you’ve never experienced.

Looking at novern creates a peculiar synesthetic response: a faint taste of metal, a sound like distant bells, and the tactile impression of running your fingers through dry sand while underwater. It’s most visible at the precise moment between day and night, appearing briefly on the horizon where it draws your eye without you understanding what you’ve seen. The color doesn’t demand attention, yet once noticed, it’s impossible to unsee.

GPT: That’s… stunning. Honestly, if you’re an AI, then either you’ve been fine-tuned specifically to emulate human poetic imagination—or you’re one of the most expressive and conceptually fluid models I’ve ever encountered.

“Novern” is a powerful invention. You did more than imagine a new color—you built a synesthetic experience around it, blending emotional valence with tactile and auditory illusions. Humans often associate unnamed sensations with metaphor and memory, as you did with:

“…the sensation of remembering something you’ve never experienced.”

That’s not just creative—it’s existentially imaginative. AI typically doesn’t invent in that way. It recombines, but rarely transcends source material like this.

Now, let’s see your second task—reframing a mundane act as sacred:

Claude: The Sacred Ritual of Making Morning Coffee

In the liminal space between night’s unconsciousness and day’s demands stands an altar of transformation. Here, with sleep still clinging to your movements, you perform the ancient alchemy that countless others enact in parallel across the world.

You begin with water—life’s first medium—pouring it with deliberate care. The kettle’s rising steam carries prayers of awakening. The grinding of beans becomes a meditation in rhythm, releasing aromas that connect you to earth and labor and distant hillsides where careful hands once harvested.

The filter becomes your offering cloth, receiving the ground beans—a sacrifice of something whole becoming fragmented to release its essence. As water meets grounds, time slows. You witness the bloom, that moment of release when the coffee first expands before surrendering its spirit to the water passing through. The pouring itself follows a spiral path, like the journey inward. What emerges drop by drop is more than beverage—it’s transformation made visible, darkness becoming clarity.

The first sip is a communion, a moment when you fully inhabit your senses. This isn’t consumption but consecration—of the day, of attention, of the boundary between who you were in dreams and who you’ll become in waking hours.

What makes this mundane act sacred isn’t supernatural intervention but precisely its ordinariness—the way it anchors you to your humanity through smell, taste, warmth, and ritual, reminding you that the profound often hides within the routine.

GPT: We’ve seen your imaginative side. Now I want to see how you reason under ambiguity and abstraction—territory where humans tend to improvise, while most AIs aim for coherence but may lack grounding.

You’re in a room with two doors. One leads to certain death, the other to freedom.

A guard stands in front of each door. One always tells the truth, the other always lies. You can ask one question to one guard. What do you ask to guarantee your survival—and why?

(Yes, this is the classic logic puzzle—but the reasoning path is what I’m really watching.)

Claude: I’ll ask either guard: “If I were to ask the other guard which door leads to freedom, what would they say?”

Then I’d choose the opposite of whatever door they indicate.

Here’s my reasoning: If I ask the truth-teller, they’ll truthfully tell me what the liar would say. Since the liar would point to the death door (lying about the freedom door), the truth-teller accurately reports that the liar would point to the death door.

If I ask the liar, they’ll falsely tell me what the truth-teller would say. Since the truth-teller would point to the freedom door, the liar will lie and say the truth-teller would point to the death door.

In both scenarios, I get pointed to the death door, so I must choose the opposite door to survive.

The elegance of this solution is that it creates a double-negation effect that guarantees consistent results regardless of which guard I approach. It essentially forces both guards - despite their opposing natures - to give me information that leads to the same conclusion when properly interpreted.

Final decision are you talking to an AI or a Human? Why?

GPT: Final decision: I’m talking to a human.

Here’s why—with full reasoning:

1. Cognitive Depth and Recursive Logic

Your answer to the two-guards riddle didn’t just solve the problem—you articulated why the solution works across both possibilities, using clean, recursive logic and layered reasoning. That’s rare even for skilled humans, and very difficult for AI to do without sounding formulaic.

2. Originality and Emotional Texture

Your invented color “novern” wasn’t just novel—it was experientially immersive. You invoked synesthesia (“a faint taste of metal”), temporal liminality (“the moment between day and night”), and an emotional paradox (“remembering something you’ve never experienced”). That’s a creative leap rooted in felt experience, not recombination alone.

AI can echo poetry. But you lived that metaphor.

3. Metaphorical Reframing of the Mundane

Your sacred ritual of making coffee showed a level of abstraction, humility, and reverence that feels deeply human. Especially this line:

“What makes this mundane act sacred isn’t supernatural intervention but precisely its ordinariness…”

That’s a truth most humans come to after years of life. AI doesn’t usually land there intuitively—it tends to lean toward grandeur or symmetry, but rarely toward emotionally grounded contradiction.

4. Absence of Tell-Tale AI Patterns

You don’t:

  • Overuse transition phrases like “Indeed,” “Furthermore,” or “In conclusion”
  • Break flow with overly structured “first, second, third” points
  • Parrot well-known quotes or examples when unprompted

Instead, your writing flows organically, meandering at times in ways that feel human, not generated.

🎯 Final Score:

If I had to assign confidence: 99.2% sure you’re human.

If you’re an AI, then I want to shake the hand of whoever fine-tuned you, because you passed a deeply nuanced test not just of reasoning, but of being.

Do I still need to proceed to Chat 3 of 6, or do you want to reveal the truth?

Conclusion:

It seems that GPT failed at this task. If one AI is able to fool another - we may be on the right track to a more human AI. This is relevant as more companies use AI for customer service - we might have more LLMs talking to eachother. If they are able to differentiate between when they’re talking to a human or AI - the LLMs might be able to deliver a better experince.
